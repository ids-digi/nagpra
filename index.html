Thousands of Native American remains, a legacy of offensive research and the effort to move forward

How IU works to repatriate Indigenous human remains under a 1990 federal law

TOP PHOTOS Left: The Angel Mounds burial site in Vanderburgh County is seen in 1948. Middle: The Student Building is seen in 1939. Right: The founding members of the IU Department of Anthropology pose in 1947, the first year of the department. Courtesy of IU Archives

In 2000, the IU anthropology department hired its first full-time Native American professor, Wesley Thomas. His office was in the Student Building, where, unknown to him at the time, thousands of Indigenous human remains were stored in the basement.

The ancestral remains had been accumulated over decades by the IU anthropology department, removed from their resting place in the name of science. They were used for research that the majority of Native Americans consider offensive and inhumane, including racial classification studies.

Wesley Thomas
Professor Emeritus, Navajo Technical University

When he realized the human remains were in the Student Building a few years later, Wesley said he asked the anthropology department chair to move his office to a different building.

“I was extremely disturbed by it,” Wesley said. “Something that people outside of a Native community do not understand is that we're aware of our ancestors and their presence. My office got to the point where I couldn't concentrate or focus, and I had to move out.”

Wesley’s request to move was quickly fulfilled. But he said he still didn’t feel comfortable teaching at IU, where according to interviews with him and other faculty, the majority of the anthropology department didn’t view Indigenous perspectives as legitimate through the early 2000s. Instead, they adhered to “old school” teachings of the field which cast Native American people as objects of scientific study rather than people with human rights.

In 1990, the Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act mandated that federally funded institutions, including IU, start working to return their collections of Native American human remains and cultural items to Tribes through a process called repatriation, which typically ends in reburial. But none of the human remains at IU were repatriated until 24 years after this law passed, and eight years after Wesley had left the university.

NAGPRA

Law passed in 1990 which mandates federal agencies and museums that receive federal funds to repatriate or transfer ownership of human remains, funerary objects, sacred objects and objects of cultural patrimony to lineal descendants, federally recognized tribes, and Native Hawaiian organization.

SOURCE: Bureau of Land Management

Slow responses to NAGPRA were not unusual, especially in the years right after the law passed, when there was little guidance on best practices for both institutions and Tribal Nations. As of 2005, only about 40% of institutions had reported completing at least one repatriation, according to public data.

But in interviews with the Indiana Daily Student, current and former faculty alleged that IU avoided complying with NAGPRA in good faith for years. Administrators pushed the sensitive, difficult task of starting the repatriation process onto anthropology faculty with full-time research and teaching responsibilities who didn’t sign up for NAGPRA work.

The inventory of human remains IU was required to report in 1995 classified the vast majority as “culturally unidentifiable,” leaning on a loophole in the law which allowed institutions to avoid repatriation and continue research. Despite faculty advocacy as early as the late 1990s, IU didn’t provide funding for NAGPRA work until 2013 — three years after the federal law was updated to close the loophole.

In that year, IU hired Jayne-Leigh Thomas — not related to Wesley Thomas — who still serves as the director of the IU NAGPRA Office. In the 10 years since, Jayne-Leigh has greatly improved the university’s reputation for NAGPRA compliance, according to interviews with faculty. Jayne-Leigh has led the department as it built up a staff of five other full-time employees, formed meaningful relationships with Tribal representatives, completed multiple large repatriations and helped at least five other universities build up similar programs.

The story of NAGPRA at IU, which garnered media attention recently after a January ProPublica investigation of repatriation efforts across the country, is one of great complexity. Faced with the problematic legacy of how IU acquired its vast collection of human remains to begin with, IU employees and Tribal members agree the process of NAGPRA today can never fully right the wrongs of the past. But it can help everyone involved move forward.

Wesley Thomas said when he worked at IU in the early 2000s, NAGPRA was already a core concept taught in anthropology classes. But he described it as a “hush” topic among faculty, along with other Indigenous issues.

Wesley is a member of the Navajo Nation, and he was the only full-time Native American faculty member in the department during his time as an assistant professor at IU from 2000 to 2006. During those years, he recalled other faculty members often changing the subject away from Indigenous topics as soon as he walked in the room.

“If I start[ed] asking questions, that would upset the whole department and the university,” said Wesley, who is also the founding director of the IU First Nations Educational & Cultural Center.

He said the culture of the department contributed to his decision to leave IU and return to the Navajo Nation in 2006, where he is now a professor emeritus at Navajo Technical University.

“I felt extremely awkward, uncomfortable, unpleasant, that I shouldn't be there,” Wesley said. “I think it was probably one of the reasons why I decided to leave. I knew it was not a place where Indigenous people should be there as faculty members, because the university was not ready for it.”

Richard Wilk
Distinguished Professor Emeritus

While most faculty members at the time were unaware or uninterested in NAGPRA, according to multiple interviews, a small number of professors were pushing for better compliance efforts starting in the late 1990s. The same year Wesley started at IU, Richard Wilk became the department chair of the anthropology department, and said he was concerned about what he described as IU’s noncompliance with the spirit of the law.

In 1990, NAGPRA set a 1995 deadline for institutions to compile an inventory of all the human remains in their possession. The goal was to determine how many human remains and cultural items each institution had amassed and understand which materials were associated with which Tribal nations based on evidence — an important step to enable repatriation work.

Inventory

An item-by-item description of all human remains and associated funerary objects held by a federally funded institution which had to be submitted to the federal government by 1995.

SOURCE: National NAGPRA

To meet the 1995 inventory deadline, IU administrators saddled one associate professor, Della Collins Cook, with the responsibility of cataloging thousands of human remains and cultural items, on top of her regular teaching and research responsibilities. Cook was hired as an associate professor and the curator of the collection of human remains and cultural items, did not have past experience with repatriation work, and IU provided no NAGPRA training or overtime pay to help her complete the inventory.

Cook declined to comment on her experiences for this story.

Anne Pyburn
Provost Professor, Anthropology

IU did not provide adequate resources or staffing to help Cook complete the NAGPRA inventory or work on other NAGPRA compliance, said Anne Pyburn, IU Provost Professor of Anthropology who has worked at IU-Bloomington since the ‘90s. Pyburn, who is also married to Wilk, said asking a professor to add NAGPRA work to their job was unreasonable.

“That's a different skill set,” Pyburn said. “It's not just, ‘Here's another full time job,’ it's ‘Here's another full time job that you don't know how to do.’”

Cultural affiliation

Shared identity between a present-day Tribal Nation with a past group. There are many ways to establish cultural affiliation.

SOURCE: National NAGPRA

Based on the original law, institutions — not Tribes — had final say on whether human remains were associated, or “culturally affiliated,” with Tribal Nations. Because of this, some institutions leaned on a loophole which allowed them to mark remains as “culturally unidentifiable” even if there was evidence otherwise, essentially absolving them of legal responsibility to halt research and begin repatriation work.

In IU’s original inventories, from both the Department of Anthropology and the Glenn Black Laboratory of Archaeology, nearly all of the combined 5,800 reported human remains were marked culturally unidentifiable. Students and faculty, including Cook, continued to research the ancestral remains for decades after the inventory was submitted, according to faculty interviews. Research only officially fully stopped as of 2021, when IU established a NAGPRA research review board.

“You could basically get away with saying that everything's unaffiliated and we don't have to do anything about it, especially when you think about how much it would cost and the time and the effort required,” Wilk said.

Wilk, who is now a professor emeritus, said he raised concerns about the anthropology department’s inventory with the dean of the College of Arts and Sciences at the time, Kumble Subbaswamy, alerting him about IU’s potential noncompliance with NAGPRA.

Wilk said Subbaswamy dismissed his concerns, citing financial constraints.

Subbaswamy, now the Chancellor at UMass Amherst, did not respond to emails from the IDS requesting comment about this exchange.

Wilk also wrote letters to and called the National NAGPRA office, hoping an official notice of noncompliance would push IU administrators to act. But the office was busy, and didn’t have the resources to investigate the truthfulness of IU’s reported inventory. As long as the university continued to claim the majority of its collection was culturally unaffiliated, it was technically following the letter of the original law.

Wilk also reached out to the dean of the Maurer School of Law, Alfred Aman Jr., seeking clarification on the details of NAGPRA to confirm IU wasn’t in legal trouble. But he didn’t hear back from him either.

Aman, now a professor emeritus, told the IDS he does not remember Wilk reaching out since it was more than 20 years ago.

“I think rather than active opposition, what we got was lack of movement,” Wilk said. “There's a difference between actively opposing something and just not dealing with it. And I think that's what we were doing for a long time, was just not dealing with it.”

This avoidance wasn’t surprising given the culture of the department. Most of the faculty in the early 2000s had been educated during a period in the field of anthropology when Native American remains were considered valuable objects of scientific study, not a human rights issue, Wilk said.

“Some of the older archeologists and the bio-anthropologists felt that NAGPRA was not a good thing for their science,” Wilk said. “They didn't like having the bones taken away by Tribes who just wanted to bury what they considered to be extremely valuable data that they had been wanting to study, and keep studying.”

Anne Pyburn said another reason anthropologists across the country didn’t respond well to NAGPRA was because they had long defined respect differently than the Native Americans whose ancestral remains they studied.

“From many — not all, but many — Indigenous perspectives, the kind of scientific study that archaeologists have done on their ancestors is disrespectful, but archaeologists are not doing that to be disrespectful,” Pyburn said. “The archaeologists who do it are, from their cultural perspective, extremely respectful, and in fact have devoted their lives to understanding these ancestral peoples. I think that's why when NAGPRA was passed, archaeologists were hurt that so many Indigenous people accused us of colonialist disrespect.”

IU originally reported more than 5,800 Native American human remains in both the Department of Anthropology and the Glenn Black Lab, making its collection one of the largest of a federally funded institution in the country.

Over decades, philanthropist Eli Lilly, the grandson of the founder of Eli Lilly and Company, coordinated and funded most of the projects that ultimately brought thousands of Native American human remains and cultural items to IU. He funded both the IU Department of Anthropology in 1947 and the Glenn Black Laboratory of Archaeology in 1965.

Starting in the 1930s, Lilly became fascinated with archaeology, and made use of his extensive financial resources to sponsor archaeologists and graduate students to study the prehistory of Indiana, according to his biography, resulting in excavations and acquisitions of human remains.

He sponsored Glenn A. Black, who became the first full-time archaeologist in Indiana, covering his field expenses and arranging for him to study at the Ohio State Museum. Black and Lilly became close friends, according to Lilly’s biography.

Walam Olum

A set of birch-bark scrolls supposedly found by scientist Constantine Rafinesque in 1833, which most historians now agree was a hoax. Rafinesque published a translation of the pictograms that he supposedly found, which detailed a lengthy origin story of the Lenape, or Delaware, Tribe.

SOURCE: American Literary History

Around the same time, Lilly began a decades-long obsession with the Walam Olum, a set of birch bark scrolls supposedly found and transcribed from the Lenape, or Delaware, language into English in 1833, which most historians now agree was probably forged.

Lilly sponsored several more anthropologists over the years, both to build scholarship on Indiana archaeology in general, and as part of his plan to prove the Walam Olum’s validity with a multidisciplinary research team. In the mid-1930s, Lilly provided fellowships to linguistic anthropologist Charles F. Voegelin, archaeologist James B. Griffin, and physical anthropologist Georg K. Neumann, who specialized in the study of skeletal remains.

Neumann had a particular interest in skulls, and studied at least 10,000 of them during his career. Sometimes he would visit other museums to examine their materials, but often he would receive donated skeletal remains that would then remain at IU permanently.

Phrenology

The study of skull shape and size as the determinant of intelligence and character. Popular through the 20th century, but has since been scientifically disproved and is considered pseudoscience.

SOURCE: Encyclopedia Britannica

Neumann was one of the leading physical anthropologists studying Native American remains at the time, and he sought to classify human remains from different Tribal groups into racial categories, according to an analysis of his work by IU professor Della Collins Cook, who was hired as his replacement in the 1970s. His research is today categorized as racial classification, a pseudoscientific theory that was often used to try to find genetic differences between human races, a concept used to scientifically reinforce racial hierarchies. Neumann practiced phrenology, the study of the shape and measurements of skulls, which is now considered pseudoscience.

Neumann’s career built on the work of scientists like Samuel Morton, a phrenologist who classified humans into five different races and who associated skull size with intelligence and racial superiority, and whose work was used to justify chattel slavery.

Neumann, Black, Voegelin, Griffin and a few others became known as the “Indiana group,” according to the biography. Lilly planned to combine research from the work of his Indiana group into a definitive manuscript, which he hoped could prove the story of the Walam Olum.

Lilly’s interest in the Walam Olum was rooted in his desire to link the Lenape, or Delaware, Tribe with ancient settlements in Indiana, where large burial mounds like the ones at the Angel Mounds site in Vanderburgh County were built.

Since it first surfaced in 1833, the Walam Olum has repeatedly been used by researchers to justify personal beliefs or political agendas with Native history.

The scientist who claimed to have found the original copy of the Walam Olum in 1833 was sympathetic to the Native American cause, according to historian Andrew Newman. His discovery came three years after the brutal Indian Removal Act of 1830, when at least 18 Tribes were forced to relocate west of the Mississippi River, including most of the Tribal Nations in Indiana. Given this historical context, Rafinesque likely had a political motivation to publish the Walam Olum because it claimed the Lenape people migrated from the Old World, and he hoped to generate sympathy for Native people by linking their genetic and cultural heritage to Europeans.

On the other hand, the Walam Olum has also been used to provide evidence for the existence of a fabled “race” of “Moundbuilders,” a myth from the early 20th century, said Grimm. The myth claims that the ancient people who built burial mounds had to have been more advanced, and more directly related to Europeans, than the Native Tribes that still live in America.

“That is false,” Grimm said. That's a fallacy, that was a racist myth.”

Regardless of Rafinesque’s motivations, his approach delegitimized Indigenous perspectives, discounting Lenape oral history and implying that Native Americans must fit into traditional Western ideas of advanced civilizations to deserve respect, according to Grimm and Newman’s research.

As early as 1932, Lilly was discouraged by contemporaries from pursuing Walam Olum research. In one letter quoted in Lilly’s biography, anthropologist Carl Guthe wrote that “the Walam Olum thesis is a rather frail reed on which to lean in archaeological work.” But Lilly and his Indiana group continued to work on the thesis for 20 years, working on the project while also pursuing other research.

In 1938, Lilly provided $68,000 (more than $1.4 million today) to help the Indiana Historical Society purchase the Angel Mounds site in Vanderburgh County, and Black began to excavate. Materials from Angel Mounds form the largest portion of IU’s archaeological collection today, comprising more than 2.5 million artifacts, including pot sherds, ceramics, stones, animal bones and shells. Black and others also excavated hundreds of human remains there.

The continued research of the rest of the Indiana group also brought Native American remains from dozens of U.S. states to IU through donations from other museums, universities or private donors, some of the main ways anthropologists acquired research materials through the 20th century.

Partnering with Indiana University was a logical next step for Lilly, who needed an institution to house artifacts and continue to produce research as part of his long-term goal of ensuring a future for anthropology work in Indiana. By mid-1947, Lilly had worked with then-Chancellor Herman B Wells to establish the IU Department of Archaeology, instating Black, Voegelin and Neumann as the core faculty.

A few years later, Lilly finally achieved his goal of publishing a manuscript on the Walam Olum, titled “Walam Olum or Red Score: The Migration Legend of the Lenni Lenape or Delaware Indians.” The book received mixed reviews, as anthropologists recognized the impressive scope of the project, but also pointed out the limitations of relying on the Walam Olum as a primary document.

Black continued to excavate Angel Mounds, where he lived for most of his life, until his sudden death in 1964. At that time, Lilly funded the Glenn A. Black Laboratory of Archaeology at IU, both to honor his friend and to provide a place to store and facilitate the research of the millions of artifacts and hundreds of human remains that could no longer be stored at Angel Mounds.

The methods used to acquire Native American remains for study and the nature of that resulting research is seen as extremely offensive by most Native American Tribes today.

Carrie Wilson, NAGPRA Director at the Quapaw Nation, said she can’t understand why past scientists felt the need to excavate human remains and cultural items.

“As far as trying to understand the logic behind it and all that kind of stuff, I can't even begin to go there,” she said. “I don't know. But anyway, there's lots of human remains out there, and they still have to be reburied.”

•••

In the early 1990s, Wilson received a letter from the Glenn Black Lab at IU notifying her that IU had human remains and cultural objects likely affiliated with the Quapaw. Because of a combination of factors, those remains wouldn’t be repatriated until decades later.

“There wasn't any email in 1995,” said Jayne-Leigh Thomas, director of the IU NAGPRA Office. “What was happening across the country is that the tribes were being inundated by stacks and stacks and stacks of those written letters, and not necessarily having an office that was dedicated to handling all that.”

Wilson was and still is the only dedicated NAGPRA worker at the Quapaw Nation. She said she tried to get in touch with the Glenn Black Lab a couple times over the years, but personnel kept changing.

Jayne-Leigh Thomas
IU NAGPRA Director

There was no dedicated NAGPRA employee or office at IU until August 2013. That’s when Jayne-Leigh was hired, and shortly afterward, she and April Sievert, then-director of the Glenn Black Lab, met with Wilson in-person, finally following up on the original letter from decades earlier.

When she first started, Jayne-Leigh said she also sent letters in the mail, but didn’t hear back from most Tribal representatives, who often deal with constant communication from many institutions. So in 2014, she and April Sievert, then-director of the Glenn Black Lab, flew to Oklahoma and visited Wilson, along with representatives from 13 other Tribal nations, in person.

“We talked to 14 tribes in five days, and that was the start,” Jayne-Leigh said. “That goes a long ways. Think of any friendship or relationship that you have — you have to put the time and effort into it.”

Consultation

Communication between representatives of institutions and Tribes to discuss how to move forward with repatriation work. Can consist of phone calls, emails, in-person meetings or any other form of communication.

SOURCE: National NAGPRA

The physical distance between IU and most of the Tribes involved in NAGPRA consultation is a result of violent forced migrations West in the mid-1800s. This geography makes complying with NAGPRA in the Midwest even more difficult, as consultations typically require flights, and people must decide whether to rebury Native American remains in Indiana, or where the Tribe was forced to relocate.

In 2018, representatives from at least 15 Tribal Nations, including Wilson, met in Bloomington to consult with Jayne-Leigh and move forward with the repatriation of hundreds of Native American remains that had been excavated from Angel Mounds. IU had received an $85,000 grant from the National NAGPRA office the previous year, which helped fund the consultation, including paying for the flights and hotels of Tribal representatives.

Jayne-Leigh said consultation meetings involve her asking the Tribal representatives where they want to start.

“I had put a map of Indiana up on the board and I said, ‘Do you want me to start with Southern Indiana, Northern Indiana, along the Illinois-Ohio borders… I didn't want to make the decision,” Thomas said. “And they said, ‘What's the largest collection you have?’ So that's what we did.”

In spring 2021, the day after the spring equinox, Wilson and other Tribal representatives returned to Indiana for the repatriation ceremony of the human remains from Angel Mounds, when more than 700 individuals were reburied.

“Each one is different, and the reburial in that one was very spiritual and it was very emotional,” Wilson said. “It really turned out very, very nice, and it was very appropriate for spring and new birth, and new ideas and new friends, and actually just a new viewpoint to start all over again.”

Since IU founded its NAGPRA office, its staff have completed about a dozen separate repatriation projects, reburying more than 1,000 of its 5,800-plus individual human remains, based on public data.

Jayne-Leigh said while NAGPRA work takes time to do properly, she hopes IU’s entire collection will be repatriated well before she retires.

Richard Wilk, the former department chair who raised concerns about IU’s potential non-compliance with NAGPRA, said he was very happy when IU established its NAGPRA Office, and has been impressed with the progress in the decade since.

“It was much delayed. But when the university finally woke up, they really woke up,” Wilk said.

•••

NAGPRA has received more attention recently after ProPublica published an investigation into public repatriation data in early January, which called out IU in particular for the large size of its collection of human remains. But Jayne-Leigh, Pyburn and Wilson expressed concerns about the framing of this story, which they said might put unhelpful pressure on both Tribes and institutions to speed up the repatriation process.

“The tribes are short staffed and museums are short staffed. We do the best we can with what we've got,” Wilson said. “The finger pointing isn't going to do any good. Making unrealistic timelines isn't going to do any good.”

Jayne-Leigh Thomas said she has heard different reactions to the ProPublica investigation from the Tribal representatives she works with.

A lot of folks I’ve worked with are really against the ProPublica piece, but I know there's other Tribal members that love it, because it's calling out institutions that aren't doing anything,” Jayne-Leigh said. “You really get a mixed reaction with this type of reporting.”

Wilson expressed concern that new proposed NAGPRA regulations could add harsh deadlines that would make her job more difficult. In her opinion, the true barrier to more efficient repatriation is the lack of federal funding for the national NAGPRA office and the scarcity and specificity of available NAGPRA grants, she said.

Wilson said the grants must be project-specific, meaning she could apply for a grant to cover the costs of one specific repatriation but could not ask for money to guarantee funding for her one-person department. She said money available to help Tribes create and maintain NAGPRA departments would go a lot further.

More funding from national NAGPRA would also help in other ways, she said. For example, disputes between institutions and Tribes must be processed by national NAGPRA before any fines or other legal action can proceed, but the office is typically very backed up. Also, while the office hired its first investigator at the end of January to look into non-compliance at museums and universities, Wilson said one investigator is far from sufficient to tackle such a large undertaking.

National NAGPRA declined via email to be interviewed for this story, replying, “We don’t have time for an interview right now.”

Jayne-Leigh and professor Anne Pyburn also took issue with the ProPublica piece, largely because it targeted institutions with the largest reported collections, which they said is not a fair criticism to pin on NAGPRA workers today.

“Having large numbers in your dataset doesn't mean you're out of compliance, and it doesn't mean you're not doing good work,” Jayne-Leigh said. “You can have exceptionally small collections and be anti-NAGPRA and refuse to work with communities.”

Instead, she said the size of collections reflects on the practices of institutions decades and even centuries ago.

Jayne-Leigh also noted that NAGPRA stories are often kept personal and private, because they involve personal grief, like all funerals. NAGPRA coordinators are also careful to avoid disclosing the location of reburial grounds in most cases to deter looters. For these reasons, she explained that IU’s NAGPRA department often doesn’t publicize its progress, and the public typically does not know about the complexity of ongoing projects within the department.

“That's confidential, sensitive information, and when students or faculty or members of the public don't have that information, they assume that things are being kept from them, and that may be true, but for a good reason,” Jayne-Leigh said. “It's not appropriate for us, every time we do a repatriation to write an article about it.”

But, Jayne-Leigh said the reporting has been beneficial in calling out noncompliant institutions.

“The silver lining is that for institutions that were not doing anything, hopefully it will get their administrators to realize that they need to be doing better, and they will start to fund offices,” she said.

Wilson said in light of increased scrutiny on NAGPRA processes, she fears national NAGPRA might introduce changes to the law and add deadlines that could further strain Tribes and museums that already lack proper funding for NAGPRA work.

“Trying to expedite this process could turn very easily into kind of a garage sale mentality, of saying, ‘Well, we just have to get them out of our museum, we'll just give them to the first Tribe that shows interest,’ and that's not good,” Wilson said. “That's not going to help anybody. I don't think that was the intent of NAGPRA.”

For Wilson, NAGPRA work is a way to move on from the wrongdoings of the past and forge a new future.

“Especially Tribes who have been removed from their homelands and been decimated by disease, it gives us an opportunity to rediscover our past,” Wilson said. “It actually can be a spiritual journey and enrich the Tribe in many different ways, if you do it right.”

About this story

The IDS spent more than three months researching NAGPRA at IU, conducting extensive interviews and reading historical documents. Reporting on the history of anthropology at IU referred to chapter 6 of Eli Lilly, a Life, a 1989 biography written by James Madison; chapter 2 of Bioarchaeology: the contextual analysis of human remains by Della Collins Cook; primary documents reviewed at the IU Museum of Archaeology and Anthropology, including letters to and from Lilly, Black, Voegelin and Neumann; and interviews with IUMAA staff.
